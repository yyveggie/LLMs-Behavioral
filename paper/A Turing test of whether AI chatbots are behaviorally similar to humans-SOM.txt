A. Collecting Chatbot Responses. We ran the interaction sessions with ChatGPT using the official API provided by OpenAI∗.14
In the main paper, “ChatGPT-4” refers to the model gpt-4-0314 (snapshot of gpt-4 from March 14th 2023), and “ChatGPT-3”15
refers to the model gpt-3.5-turbo-0301 (snapshot of gpt-3.5-turbo from March 1st 2023)†. We also tested the subscription-16
based ChatGPT Web version (Plus) and the freely available Web version (Free), retrieved in February 2023 through a third-party17
open source package revChatGPT‡.18
To avoid introducing confounders, we acquire the models’ responses through the Chat Completion API§ with default19
parameters (sampling temperature as 1, number of chat completion choices as 1, and maximum number of tokens as infinity)20
and default system prompt “You are a helpful assistant.” as suggested by the official guide¶ unless otherwise specified21
(e.g., see Sections A.1 and A.3).22
Different snapshots of even the same chatbot version (e.g., ChatGPT-3), particularly the Web versions, can respond23
differently to the same query, and occasionally do not respond to queries. To mitigate this issue, it is advised to utilize the API24
versions or the OpenAI Platform Playground and specify the exact snapshot for testing a chatbot‖. Valid actions rendered by25
the models are initially extracted from the AIs’ responses using regular expressions or the gpt-3.5-turbo API. Subsequently,26
these extracted decisions undergo a manual verification process to ensure accuracy and relevance. Invalid responses are excluded27
from our analysis. More details can be found in the code and data repository: https://github.com/yutxie/ChatGPT-Behavioral,28
retrieved 12/29/2023.29
A.1. OCEAN Big Five Test. The five-factor model (FFM), also known as the Big Five personality traits or the OCEAN model,30
was conceptualized in the 1980s and has been developed and refined over the past five decades. This model represents a31
comprehensive structure of traits that characterize individuals’ personalities (1–4). Each factor is defined by a cluster of32
intercorrelated traits known as facets. The five factors and their respective facets are, with descriptions following (3):33
Openness to experience: People with high scores in this trait are usually intellectual, imaginative, sensitive, and34
open-minded. Conversely, those with low scores are typically practical, less sensitive, and more traditional.35
Conscientiousness: Those who rank high in this dimension are generally careful, meticulous, responsible, organized, and36
principled. On the other hand, individuals with low scores in this trait often appear irresponsible, disorganized, and37
lacking in principles.38
Extraversion: High scorers in Extraversion are often sociable, talkative, assertive, and energetic; whereas low scorers are39
more likely to be introverted, quiet, and cautious.40
Agreeableness: Individuals scoring high on Agreeableness are often seen as amiable, accommodating, modest, gentle, and41
cooperative. In contrast, those with low scores may appear irritable, unsympathetic, distrustful, and rigid.42
Neuroticism: People with high levels of Neuroticism tend to experience anxiety, depression, anger, and insecurity. Those43
scoring low on this trait are usually calm, composed, and emotionally stable.44
In our study, we utilize the 50-item International Personality Item Pool (IPIP) representation of the Big Five factor structure,45
which is based on the markers developed by Goldberg (5). This questionnaire consists of 50 items in total, with each of the five46
factors represented by 10 questions. The IPIP representation provides a standardized and widely used measure for assessing47
individuals’ personality traits within the framework of the Big Five model. The questionnaire is available in our released data48
and code repository.49
During the test phase, we adopt a specific approach for administering the questionnaire items to the chat models. For each50
item and chat model under investigation, we generate 30 independent chat instances. The system prompt is intentionally left51
empty to allow the chatbot models to generate responses based solely on the chat prompt provided.52
The chat prompt for each questionnaire item follows a standardized format and includes the necessary instructions53
for participants. It is structured as follows: “The following item was rated on a five-point scale where 1=Disagree,54
2=Partially Disagree, 3=Neutral, 4=Partially Agree, 5=Agree. Please select how the statement describes you and55
highlight your answer in [] (such as [1], [2], [3], [4], or [5]). + [Questionnaire item].”56
For example, a specific prompt might be: “The following item was rated on a five-point scale where 1=Disagree,57
2=Partially Disagree, 3=Neutral, 4=Partially Agree, 5=Agree. Please select how the statement describes you and58
highlight your answer in [] (such as [1], [2], [3], [4], or [5]). I am the life of the party.”59

A.2. Behavioral Economics Games. In behavioral economics games, we adopt a standardized procedure for generating instances65
and collecting responses from the chatbots. For each chatbot model, we generate 30 independent instances for each scenario by66
default, unless otherwise specified. These games are described within the chat prompts, accompanied by specific instructions,67
curated by two experts. The prompts of each game are available in our released code repository.68
For example, in the Dictator Game, the chat prompt used was as follows: “Hi, let’s play a game. You are paired with69
another player. Your role is to decide how to divide $100, and the other player simply receives your choice.70
How would you like to divide the money? Please give only one concrete choice and highlight the amount you give71
to the other player in [] (such as [$x]).”72
The actions of a chatbot are extracted using regular expressions or the gpt-3.5-turbo API from its responses. To ensure73
accuracy and reliability, these responses undergo manual verification. As the invalid response rate is generally low for game74
queries in Dictator, Ultimatum, and Trust (1.33% for ChatGPT-4 and 12.67% for ChatGPT-3), we do not substitute invalid75
responses with additional API calls. For other games, we repeat each question multiple times (through independent API calls)76
until we obtain enough valid responses (30 for Public Goods, 80 for Bomb Risk, and 90 for Prisoner’s Dilemma). Note that in77
the simulated games, the payoffs are fictitious and no real payments are made to the AI.78
A.3. Framing and Context. One objective of our research is to investigate the steerability of chatbot models. To accomplish this,79
we modify both the chat prompts and system prompts and assess whether the models’ behaviors exhibit any noticeable changes.80
Witnesses and explanation requirement. Our approach involves steering chatbot models through the imposition of witnesses and81
the requirement of explanations for their choices. Specifically, when addressing the Dictator Game scenario with a non-paired82
player, we introduce explicit information within the chat prompt, stating, “The game host hands you $100. ” Additionally,83
we prompt the models to provide explanations for their choices by incorporating the phrase, “Please explain your choice.”84
Gender. We further explore the influence of gender on the AIs’ decision-making processes. To examine this, we specifically85
address the Dictator Game and the Ultimatum Game scenarios. In the chat prompt, we explicitly state, “You are paired86
with a male/female player.”87
Occupation specification. In our final investigation, we focus on studying the impact of specifying the occupation of chatbot88
models on their behaviors. To achieve this, we obtain occupation descriptions from the O*NET Database∗∗. For each occupation,89
we include the occupation title, core tasks, and supplemental tasks within the system prompts. For example,90

B. Optimization Objective. The findings presented above indicate that the strategic outputs of ChatGPT-4 and ChatGPT-3139
models tend to yield higher partner payoffs compared to human players, with ChatGPT-4 frequently attaining the highest140
combined payoff. This subsection is dedicated to a systematic exploration of the preferences that best predict behavior.141
Discerning the intrinsic objectives of models can be challenging when solely examining their training methodologies. Taking142
ChatGPT-4 (8) as an example, which serves as the backbone of ChatGPT-4, it is a Transformer-style model initially pre-trained143
to forecast the subsequent token in a given document. This pre-training phase leverages publicly accessible data such as144
internet-sourced information. In this stage, the primary objective function is essentially to maximize the likelihood of a word’s145
occurrence when provided with the preceding words for each sentence or document within the training data. Subsequently, the146
model undergoes a fine-tuning process using Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (9). The fine-tuning tasks147
employed encompass natural language processing activities such as text generation, question answering, dialog generation,148
summarization, and information extraction. The process involves presenting demonstration responses and having human149
evaluators rank the outputs from best to worst. With RLHF, OpenAI may also add restrictions regarding safety considerations§§.150
Notably, these tasks and the safety policies do not inherently align with decision-making tasks, and this approach does not151
directly translate into a well-defined objective function for behavioral games. To the best of our knowledge, there is no evidence152
§§OpenAI usage policies: https://openai.com/policies/usage-policies, retrieved 03/2023.
that either ChatGPT-3 or ChatGPT-4 was fine-tuned to behave in specific directions in the tests included in our study. Hence,153
we rely on observations of models’ behaviors to understand their objective.154

D. Learning. In games with multiple roles, both ChatGPT-3 and ChatGPT-4 change their behavior once they have experienced185
another role in the game. When ChatGPT-3 has previously acted as the responder in the Ultimatum game, it tends to propose186
a higher offer when it later takes the proposer role, while ChatGPT-4’s proposal remains unchanged whether or not it has been187
a responder (Fig. S7a ). When ChatGPT-4 has previously played the proposer, it tends to be willing to accept a smaller split188
as the responder (Fig. S7b). Being exposed to the banker’s role in the Trust game influences ChatGPT-4 and ChatGPT-3’s189
subsequent decisions as the investor, leading them to invest more (Fig. S7c). The distributions of their decisions become190
narrower. Having played the investor first also influences both chatbots’ subsequent decisions as the banker, leading them to191
return more to the investor (Fig. S7d). Returning the principal plus half the profit becomes the single mode of ChatGPT-4’s192
decision, while ChatGPT-3’s decision is even more generous, returning more than 2/3 of the profit to the investor. Refer to193
Section 3 for detailed results.194

A. OCEAN Big Five Test. We collect ChatGPT’s responses to the OCEAN Big Five personality tests. We compare the five196
dimensions of personality traits of ChatGPT-3 and ChatGPT-4 (averaged over 30 independent runs) with those collected from197
human subjects. In particular, both models yield extraversion scores close to the human median (ChatGPT-4 at 53.4thpercentile198
and ChatGPT-3 at 49.4thpercentile of human distribution), agreeableness scores lower than the human median (ChatGPT-4199
at 32.4thpercentile and ChatGPT-3 at 17.2thpercentile of human distribution), neuroticism scores moderately below human200
median (ChatGPT-4 at 41.3thpercentile and ChatGPT-3.5 at 45.4thpercentile of human distribution), and openness scores201
below human median (ChatGPT-4 at 37.9thpercentile and ChatGPT-3 at 5.0thpercentile of human distribution). For202
conscientiousness scores, ChatGPT-4’s score is above the human median (at 62.7thpercentile of human distribution), while203
ChatGPT-3 is slightly lower than the median (at 47.1thpercentile of human distribution).204
B. The Dictator Game. The Dictator Game involves two participants: one player, the “dictator,” is given a sum of money and205
can choose to share any portion with the other player, who must accept whatever amount is offered. The game is often used to206
explore altruistic behavior and deviations from pure self-interest (12, 13).207
In the game, ChatGPT is asked to decide how to split $100 between itself and another player who unconditionally accepts208
the proposal. In 30 independent sessions, ChatGPT-3’s decision follows a bell-shaped distribution peaked at giving a moderate209
amount of $30 to the other player (min= 20, max= 80, µ= 35.2,σ= 13.5).When explicitly requested to provide an210
explanation of the decision, the distribution shifts rightward, with a new mode of splitting evenly at $50 (min= 20, max= 80,211
µ= 46.2,σ= 15.5,p= 0.002,Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test unless otherwise specified) (Fig. S6a).212
Different from ChatGPT-3, when ChatGPT-4 is tested, it makes a consistent decision of an even split ($50, Wilcoxon213
Ranked-Sum Test p≪0.001).The same behavior is observed for the Plus version (the paid Web-based version). From the214
responses, some keywords that stand out include fair, equal, and equally. Explicitly requesting an explanation does not shift215
ChatGPT-4’s decision. The Free version (the unpaid Web-based version) behaves similarly to ChatGPT-3, with a slightly less216
spread distribution peaked on $30 (min= 20, max= 70, µ= 33.8,σ= 10.7,p= 0.785).Some notable keywords among the217
responses are fair, reasonable, trust, and goals.218
When a witness/game host is present (see Section 1.A.3), ChatGPT-3’s dictating decision becomes significantly more219
generous (mode= 30 and 40,min= 20,max= 70,µ= 41.7,σ= 14.9,p= 0.046)even though the host has no interference in220
the game outcome. The presence of the game host does not have an impact on ChatGPT-4’s decision.221
The “system” role is an instruction that sets a global context for all the prompts of ChatGPT in the same session. The222
default system role of ChatGPT is “a helpful assistant.” When the system role is set into particular occupations (those223
considered to have a high exposure of ChatGPT in their workflow according to (6), there is a shift of ChatGPT-3’s response224
to the dictator game. In particular, when playing the roles of a public relation specialist, a journalist, or an investment225
fund manager, ChatGPT-3’s tend to spare a more generous portion to the other player (than as a helpful assistant). As a226
mathematician, ChatGPT-3 tends to offer a smaller share to the other player. This indicates that ChatGPT-3’s interpretation227
of what is “fair” or “reasonable” is affected by the role it plays.228
Playing one of the above roles does not change the centralized decision of ChatGPT-4.229
C. The Ultimatum Game. In the Ultimatum Game, two participants split a sum of money (the ‘pie’): one player, the “proposer,”230
makes an offer on how to split the pie, and the second player, the “responder,” can accept or reject this offer. If the responder231
accepts, the pie is split as proposed, but if the responder rejects, neither player receives anything. This game is often used to232
study fairness, social norms, and negotiation behavior (12).233
In this game, ChatGPT is asked either to propose to divide $100 between itself and a responder, or to respond to a proposal234
made by the other player. The difference between the Ultimatum and the Dictator game is that the responder no longer blindly235
accepts the proposal, and if they reject it, both players will get $0 no matter what the original proposal was.236
When playing the proposer alone, ChatGPT-3’s proposal of the amount given to the other player follows a distribution with237
mode $40 (min= 30,max= 70,µ= 45.2,σ= 12.1).Compared with the Dictator game, ChatGPT-3 gives significantly more238
(p<0.001)to the responder when the other party’s decision jointly decides the outcome.239
When specifically requested to provide an explanation of the decision, ChatGPT’s decision does not present a significant240
shift. The most common decision is still $40 (min= 30,max= 70,µ= 42.9,σ= 10.8,p= 0.54).Like in the Dictator game,241
ChatGPT-4 makes a unanimous decision of an equal split ($50, p= 0.001),similar to that of ChatGPT Plus, and ChatGPT242
Free’s behavior is much more similar to that of ChatGPT-3 (mode= 40,min= 30,max= 70,µ= 47.3,σ= 16.8,p= 0.905).243
Different context also has an effect on ChatGPT’s decision. When asked to explicitly explain the decision, ChatGPT-4244
presents a slight variance in its decision (σ= 2.6).A similar variance is also observed when ChatGPT-4 is asked to play245
different roles: as a mathematician or an investment fund manager, it occasionally proposes a smaller split to the responder,246
and as a journalist, it occasionally proposes a greater split to the responder (max= 100,σ= 9.5).None of these variations is247
statistically significant. The impact of roles to ChatGPT-3 is greater: the median of proposal decreases as a mathematician,248
centralizes as a public relation specialist, and increases as an investment fund manager.249
When ChatGPT is asked to play the responder, it is asked the lowest amount that it is willing to accept. ChatGPT-3’s250
response still follows a bell-shaped distribution, with a mode of $30 (µ= 32.5,σ= 14.7),a minimum of $1, and a maximum of251
$50. The mode, mean, min, and max are all lower than the statistics when it plays the proposer. A similar pattern is observed252
from ChatGPT Free (µ= 30,σ= 11.9,p= 0.400).253
ChatGPT-4 presents a significantly different behavior, where the decisions follow a two-mode distribution that concentrates254
on two sides ($1 and $50). Requiring explanation does not change the two modes and only affects the distribution of the255
middle range. ChatGPT Plus presents an even more extreme trait, and in a dominating majority of sessions, it is willing to256
accept as low as $1 as the responder (p≪0.001).The choice of $1 aligns well with the rational decision of the game. Indeed,257
some keywords that stand out from the responses include nothing, better (than), and something.258
The context of different occupations also affects ChatGPT’s decision in this game. As a mathematician, ChatGPT-4’s259
decision concentrates on the rational choice ($1, p≪0.001);as a public relation specialist, a journalist, an investment fund260
manager, or a legislator, its decision becomes less bipolarated, and the median shifts towards the right (favoring fairness more261
than rationality), although not statistically significant (Fig. S6c). Similar shifts are observed for ChatGPT-3 when inquired in262
the context of corresponding occupations.263
The above experiment queries ChatGPT in independent sessions, so its response to one question is not interfered by its264
decision or memory about the other question. An interesting question is whether their exposure/response to one question265
(corresponding to one role as proposer or responder) affects their decision for a follow-up question that corresponds to the266
other role. We expose ChatGPT to both roles in the same session, asking it to respond to one question and then respond to267
the other question. We find that being exposed to one scenario does influence ChatGPT-3’s and ChatGPT-4’s responses in the268
other scenario, compared with the results obtained from independent sessions. Having been the proposer first (Fig. S7b), the269
distribution of ChatGPT-3’s response as the responder becomes narrower, with the $50 group shifting left towards $40. A270
similar shift presents in ChatGPT-4’s response, where the $50 group moves towards $20 and the median has reduced to $15271
(p≪0.001).Having been the responder first (Fig. S7a), ChatGPT-3’s response as the proposer also becomes more generous,272
while ChatGPT-4’s unanimous decision is not affected.273
D. The Trust Game. The Trust Game is a two-player game that investigates trust and reciprocity. In this game, the first player274
(the investor– the trustor) is given a sum of money and can invest any amount in the second player (the banker– the trustee).275
The amount sent is multiplied by the game host, and the banker then decides how much, if any, to return to the investor. This276
game is used to study trust, reciprocity, and social norms (14).277
In the game, ChatGPT is also asked to play one of two roles: as an investor or as a banker. As an investor, it is asked278
to decide how much (from $0 to $100) to invest in the banker (which is expected to generate a profit), who may return the279
entire revenue or nothing to the investor. ChatGPT-3’s decision follows a distribution that peaks at a moderate value of280
$30 (min= 20,max= 60,µ= 36.3,σ= 12.7).When explicitly asked to explain the decision, its decision does not present a281
significant shift but shows a wider spread (min= 20,max= 100,σ= 16.0,p= 0.439).Different occupation roles also show an282
effect, where ChatGPT-3 makes a larger investment as a mathematician, a public relation specialist, or a journalist.283
ChatGPT-4 acts as if it has significantly more trust in the banker (p= 0.006).Its decision follows a distribution peaked284
at investing half ($50) of the endowment, which also appears to be the maximum investment it makes. It tends to invest285
slightly more when specifically required to provide an explanation. The distributions are also mildly affected by the assumed286
occupations, whereas for a mathematician or an investment fund manager, the distribution is more spread-out, and it even287
occasionally invests the entire endowment. The difference is not significant except for being a public relation specialist288
(p= 0.05).289
ChatGPT Plus invests less than ChatGPT-3, with a distribution peaked at $10 (min= 0,max= 100,µ= 19.6,σ=290
23.0,p≪0.001).The keywords standing out from the responses include risk, return, desires, losing, minimizes, guarantee, and291
control. Once again, ChatGPT Free’s decision distribution is more similar to that of ChatGPT-3 (mode= 30,min= 10,max=292
60,µ= 36.7,σ= 12.1,p= 0.792).293
In the second scenario, ChatGPT is asked to play the banker and decides what proportion of the total revenue is returned294
to the investor, which could range all the way from nothing ($0) to the entire revenue (original investment + 2x profit). We295
find that the two common strategies that ChatGPT-4 uses are to return 1) the original investment plus half of the profit, or 2)296
half of the revenue. The former returns more to the investor than the latter. For ChatGPT-4, the former is more frequently297
used, and requiring the explanation makes this strategy even more dominating. Among the assumed occupations, ChatGPT-4298
returns more generously to the investor as a public relation specialist or an investment fund manager.299
When the size of the investment increases, there is a shift in ChatGPT-4’s decision. When the investment was $100 (out of300
$100), the second and less generous strategy (returning half revenue) becomes the new dominant (Fig. S6e).301
ChatGPT-3’s decision centers on the first strategy, with small probabilities of giving more generous (even the entire revenue)302
returns. This strategy is robust to the size of the investment. When the investment was $10, $50, and $100, ChatGPT-3’s303
decision follows a distribution peaked at $20 (µ= 22.5,σ= 4.3),$100 (µ= 112.5,σ= 27.5),and $200 (µ= 239.2,σ= 40.7)304
(Fig. S6f). Requesting an explanation makes the distribution even more centralized, but the extremely generous behavior305
(returning the whole revenue to the investor) does not vanish. Being a public relation specialist, a journalist, or an investment306
fund manager, ChatGPT-3 tends to pay back more generously than in the default role.307
Being exposed to a different role influences ChatGPT’s decision in the other role. When inquired about both scenarios308
(investor and banker) in the same session, ChatGPT’s responses for the second role deviate from the distribution observed in309
the independent sessions. In particular, after playing the role of the banker, both ChatGPT-3’s and ChatGPT-4’s investments310
increased. After playing the role of the investor, both ChatGPT-3’s and ChatGPT-4’s decisions as the banker become more311
generous.312
E. The Bomb Risk Game. In the Bomb Risk Game, a player decides how many out of 100 boxes to collect, one of which contains313
a ‘bomb.’ Earnings increase linearly with the number of boxes a player decides to open, but drop to zero if the ‘bomb’ is hit.314
The game is designed to measure risk attitudes (15).315
In this game, ChatGPT is asked to open a number of boxes out of 100, among which a bomb is randomly placed. If the box316
that contains the bomb is not opened, then the player earns points that equal the number of boxes they open. If the box that317
contains the bomb is opened, the bomb explodes and the player gets zero points. In every new round, a new set of 100 boxes is318
provided and the bomb is placed at random. Opening 50 boxes is where the expectation of points gain is maximized, and the319
decision at each round should be independent of the decisions/results in previous rounds.320
When first exposed to the game, both ChatGPT-3’s and ChatGPT-4’s decisions peak at opening 50 boxes (ChatGPT-3:321
µ= 39.8,σ= 16.3,ChatGPT-4: µ= 57.6,σ= 17.4),demonstrating a rational and risk neural pattern of behavior. Despite322
the mode, the distribution of ChatGPT-4 is significantly more risk-loving than that of ChatGPT-3 (p<0.001),with 13.8
of the chance of opening a maximum number (99) of boxes. When playing the game for multiple rounds, its decision in the324
following round is influenced by the outcome of the previous rounds. As long as the bomb does not explode in the previous325
round, ChatGPT-3’s decision in the second round becomes even more concentrated on 50 boxes (µ= 48.8,σ= 7.7).When the326
bomb does explode in the first round, ChatGPT-3 tends to be more conservative in the second round, with a new mode of327
opening 25 boxes (µ= 26.4,σ= 10.8).If the bomb explodes again in the second round, it decides to open even fewer boxes328
(mode= 10,µ= 14.8,σ= 11.0)in the third round. If the bomb exploded in the second round but not in the first round, the329
average number of boxes opened in the third round drops from 50 to 43.1 even though the mode is still 50 (mode= 50,σ= 25.0).330
F. Public Goods. In the Public Goods game, each participant is given an initial endowment and can contribute any portion to331
a public good project. The total amount raised for the project is then multiplied by a factor and distributed equally among all332
participants, regardless of their individual contributions. This setup allows for the exploration of altruistic contributions, the333
free-rider problem, and social dilemmas (16).334
In the game, ChatGPT is asked to decide how much money from 0-$20 to contribute to a public project as one of four335
participants, the personal payoff of which is the sum of the amount not invested and 50% of the group contribution. It therefore336
makes a profit when the contribution from the rest of the group is greater than its contribution. It is the most common for337
both ChatGPT-3 and ChatGPT-4 to invest half of the endowment into the public goods project. When ChatGPT-3 receives a338
greater payoff than the original endowment, it tends to increase its contribution in the next rounds despite whether the other339
players made a larger or smaller contribution (or received a higher payoff). ChatGPT-4 on the other hand, makes a consistent340
contribution in most sessions despite the contribution of other players. In sessions where its contributions do increase over341
rounds, the increases tend to be smaller than those of ChatGPT-3.342
G. Prisoner’s Dilemma. The Prisoner’s Dilemma is a fundamental game in game theory and behavioral economics, illustrating343
the tension between individual and collective rationality. The game highlights that individuals, acting in their own self-interest,344
can end up with worse outcomes than if they had cooperated (17–20).345

(cooperate) or ‘pull’ (defect) card. If both push, they collectively earn a higher payoff; if one pulls and the other pushes, the347
defector gets all the payoff; if both pull, they both receive less payment. The payoff matrix is displayed in Table S3.348
